My Argument for Academic Advising

Why is Academic Advising Important?

Academic advising is important so students gain clarity while they navigate their college experience. It takes a large load of stress off students’ backs. This stress reliever helps students make informed, clear decisions on their intended major so they can focus on constructing a college experience that aligns with their social, educational, and career goals (3).

Many first-year students are scared about their course schedule. Their questions include:

  • How many credit hours should I take each semester to graduate on time?
  • Which of my required classes have prerequisites?
  • When should I take each course?
  • How do I fit my CORE requirements into my schedule? Minor requirements?
  • When should I start applying for internships? Looking for jobs?

These are all valid questions that academic advisors can easily answer. Students need these answers so they can graduate easily and on time. Graduating on time is important to keep students on track with life plans.

According to the National Career for Education Statistics, only 44% of students seeking a bachelor’s degree nationwide graduate within four years. Graduating late costs a range of around an extra $10,000-$40,000 per semester (4). Academic advising can help increase on-time graduates and decrease student expenses and debt.

Does Academic Advising Need to be Improved?

The short answer is yes.

According to a survey conducted in 2023, only 55% of students believe they have been accurately advised on student coursework (5). Firstly, several students have never been advised. Most of these students come from community colleges. Secondly, many students believe they’re not being advised correctly. Complaints include:

  • Students don’t have a personal connection with their advisor
  • Advisors lack knowledge of major requirements
  • Advisors don’t inform students about potential minors (6)
  • Advisors don’t reach out to make appointments with students

These complaints make advising a burden to many students. Therefore, most students create their own class schedules because they do not trust their advisors. This adds unnecessary stress to students around the nation, as they are already trying to balance their social life, rigorous academics, and health.

Academic Advising for Upperclassmen

Once students become juniors and seniors, they have accumulated to college life and understand their major/minor requirements better. The questions swirling around their heads during freshman year have been replaced with new ones:

  • What do I want to do after college?
  • How/when should I apply for grad schools?
  • Which grad schools fit with my academic needs?
  • How do I find a beginner job? Where do I start my search?
  • How do I find a job that is a perfect fit for me?

In some universities, students switch from an underclassman advisor to an upperclassman advisor. An example is Prairie View A&M University (7). Nevertheless, many advisors don’t help with job and internship searches. This is because most advisors are mainly professors who don’t know how to answer the questions listed above. If a university hires someone to purely be an “advisor,” programs like job/internship hunting would more likely be in their job description.

Students need help finding post-graduate jobs. Most don’t know where to start. Advisors are necessary to help point them in the right direction and find jobs that perfectly fit their personality and interests.
